Core Vision

"A living galaxy that runs whether I'm watching or not. I'm one actor in it, not the god of it. Can I build an economy simulation that actually works over years, solving the problems that killed X4, Dune Awakening, and Mount & Blade economies?"

A persistent, always-online space economy simulation running 24/7 on a server. There is no expectation of human players beyond the designer/observer. The economy IS the product: an EVE-scale living world with M&B-style delegation, where NPC factions mine, trade, build, expand, and wage war autonomously. The designer participates as one actor, flying a ship, building a faction, delegating to NPC governors and admirals, and watching emergent behavior unfold over months and years.

Design Pillars
  • Living simulation, not a game to "beat" - the galaxy runs 24/7, evolves over years, no win state beyond emergent outcomes
  • EVE-scale economy - millions of units of ore, weeks to build capital ships, real scarcity and geographic friction
  • M&B delegation model - appoint governors, fleet commanders, set policy. NPCs execute with their own competence.
  • Economy that doesn't collapse - solving the X4/Dune/M&B problem where economies spiral into surplus or starvation
  • Warfare as demand sink - ships get destroyed, must be rebuilt. Perpetual cycle prevents economic stagnation.
  • Geographic friction matters - 2,500 systems means logistics is hard. Rare resources are far away and dangerous to reach.

What You Actually Do

You are a participant in the galaxy, not the controller of it. You can fly a ship (up to a battleship or dreadnought), but direct combat piloting is not the point. The game is strategic, not twitch.

Player Activities

  • Fly your ship - trade, explore, haul cargo when you want hands-on gameplay
  • Set faction policy - expand toward these systems, prioritize these resources, build dreadnoughts not frigates
  • Appoint governors and admirals - NPCs with personalities and competence levels execute your strategy
  • Build your empire - establish stations, claim territory, raise security levels over time
  • Engage in battles (simulated) - when your fleet engages, you see a battle status window with outcomes unfolding. You can retreat, but you don't fly individual ships in combat.
  • Observe and tune - watch the economy, identify bottlenecks, make strategic corrections

Combat Model

Combat is simulated, not flown. Think M&B/Warhammer auto-resolve with visibility:

  • When your fleet engages (or is engaged), a battle status window opens
  • You see ship counts, damage, losses ticking in real-time
  • You have a Retreat button to disengage (at cost of ships covering the retreat)
  • Outcomes depend on ship classes, numbers, fittings, and admiral competence
  • No direct piloting, no dodging, no aiming. It's a strategic game.

The Living Economy

EVE-Scale Resource Requirements

Ship construction requires millions of raw ore units for capital ships. This is intentional. It means:

  • A frigate takes hours to produce from raw materials
  • A cruiser takes days
  • A battleship takes a week or more
  • A dreadnought takes 2-4 weeks of sustained production
  • Every ship destroyed in warfare is FELT economically

Key Economic Properties

  • Self-sustaining - the economy runs indefinitely without intervention. Production, consumption, and destruction balance.
  • Warfare-driven demand - ships get destroyed, creating perpetual demand for the entire production chain
  • Geographic scarcity - rare ores only exist in dangerous space. Long supply chains create meaningful logistics.
  • Self-limiting production - stations throttle based on input availability. No infinite surplus spiral.
  • Cascading disruptions - a blockade of rare ore disrupts the entire chain. One battle can crash a regional economy.

Faction Expansion and Security Evolution

The galaxy starts mostly wild. Factions hold small territories. Over months and years of real-time simulation:

  • Factions build stations in adjacent unclaimed systems
  • Infrastructure raises security level over time (none -> low -> medium -> high)
  • Warfare pushes back: systems can regress if stations are destroyed
  • The Corsairs and frontier conflicts actively resist civilization
  • Full galaxy pacification (if it ever happens) takes years of real-time

This is the long game. One system might flip security level per week on average, with setbacks. The galaxy slowly civilizes, but it's a tug-of-war that may never fully resolve.

Technical Direction

Architecture
  • Backend: Python (Flask), always-on server on fly.io (~$3.50/month)
  • Frontend: Vanilla JS + Three.js (3D star map) + HTML/CSS panels
  • Economy Engine: Real-time agent-based tick simulation (1 tick/sec)
  • Scale: 2,500 star systems, 100,000+ NPC ships (simulated abstractly)
  • Database: SQLite (game_data.db for static data, game.db for runtime state)
  • Performance: NPCs simulated as state machines (system ID + state + cargo). Only rendered when player is in-system.
  • Deployment: fly.io, auto-deploy via GitHub Actions on push to master

Implementation Phases

Phase 1: Economy Rescaling (Current)

  • Rescale all recipes to EVE-tier quantities (millions of raw ore for capitals)
  • Balance mining rates, production rates, and construction times
  • Target: frigate = hours, dreadnought = weeks
  • Ensure warfare consumption balances production

Phase 2: Faction Expansion AI

  • Factions build stations in unclaimed adjacent systems
  • Territory claims and sovereignty mechanics
  • Security level progression over time
  • NPC governors with competence ratings

Phase 3: Universe Expansion to 2,500 Systems

  • Procedural generation with hand-placed faction homeworlds
  • Proper cluster/security distribution
  • Jump gate network with chokepoints
  • Ore distribution following security gradient

Phase 4: Player Layer

  • Fly your ship (trade, explore, mine)
  • Join or create a faction
  • Delegation UI (appoint governors, admirals, set policy)
  • Battle status window with retreat option
  • Strategic map and empire overview

Phase 5: Long-term Simulation

  • Run 24/7 for months/years
  • Watch factions expand, clash, trade
  • Observe if the economy self-sustains or needs tuning
  • Add depth as needed (diplomacy, espionage, tech progression)
