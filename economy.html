Economy Model

The economy is a production chain. Raw materials flow upward through refining, manufacturing, and assembly into complex products. Every finished good has a traceable lineage back to rocks pulled out of asteroid fields.

Core Principles

  • Mining-centric: The vast majority of raw material production comes from ships (player and NPC miners), not stations. Mining colonies provide infrastructure and buying, but the ore comes from the belt.
  • Production chains: Each tier consumes goods from the tier below it. Disrupting one layer cascades upward.
  • Directional trade: Goods flow up the chain (ore toward factories) and finished products flow back out (components toward frontier). This gives trade routes natural direction.
  • No free lunch: Nothing appears from nowhere. Every unit of steel was once iron ore that a ship mined, hauled, and delivered to a refinery.
  • Tech levels: Higher tech versions of products require rarer inputs and more complex recipes. Same product category, harder to produce, more valuable.

Mining Model

Mining ships are the foundation of the economy. Without miners, refineries starve, factories halt, and shipyards go silent. The economy literally depends on people (and NPCs) flying into asteroid fields and pulling rocks out.

How Mining Works

  • Asteroid fields exist in specific systems, each with a density rating and yield list
  • Mining ships fly to a field, deploy lasers, and extract over time (tick-based)
  • Yield varies by field density, ship mining equipment tier, and some randomness
  • Cargo fills up, miner travels to nearest buyer (refinery/station) and sells
  • Fields are not depletable in v1 (infinite source, limited by mining rate)
  • Rare materials only appear in specific field types in specific regions

Mining vs Trading (Player Choice)

  • Mining: Guaranteed income, slower, predictable. The "safe" path. You always know you can mine.
  • Trading: Higher potential profit, volatile, requires market knowledge. The "smart" path.
  • Both: Mine on the way, trade the refined product. Vertical integration.

Price Dynamics

What Drives Prices

  • Supply: Current inventory at a station. More stock = lower price.
  • Demand: Consumption rate (what the station uses per tick) plus unfilled buy orders.
  • Scarcity cascade: If a refinery runs out of T1 inputs, it stops producing T2. Downstream factories starve. T3/T4 prices spike.
  • Glut: Too many miners flooding ore into a colony crashes T1 prices at that location.
  • Distance premium: Remote stations pay more because fewer haulers serve them.

Why the Economy Self-Corrects (and Doesn't)

  • Self-correcting: High prices attract more haulers. More supply arrives. Prices drop.
  • NOT self-correcting: If a chokepoint is blockaded, no amount of high prices will attract haulers who cannot get through. Scarcity persists until the blockade breaks.
  • Player opportunity: The gap between "self-correcting" and "stuck" is where you make money. You see what NPCs cannot, you take risks they avoid, you find routes they have not discovered.
